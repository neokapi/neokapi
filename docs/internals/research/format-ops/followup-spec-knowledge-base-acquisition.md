# Building a legally-safe, versioned, machine-consumable spec knowledge base for neokapi

Research date: 2026-06-11. Scope: acquisition, licensing, snapshotting, anchoring, and retrieval-unit infrastructure so AI agents can generate spec-cited conformance tests (`spec.yaml` citations `{url, anchor, quote}`).

## Findings

### 1. Per-publisher rights: what the licenses actually say

**W3C — two licenses, sharply different.**
- The **W3C Document License (2023)** ([w3.org/copyright/document-license-2023](https://www.w3.org/copyright/document-license-2023/)) grants "permission to copy, and distribute the contents of this document … in any medium for any purpose and without fee or royalty" with attribution + notice + link back, but **"the publication of derivative works of this document for use as a technical specification is expressly prohibited"** and "no right to create modifications or derivatives of W3C documents is granted" — *except* the implementation carve-out: parties "may prepare and distribute derivative works and portions of this document **in software, in supporting materials accompanying software, and in documentation**". That carve-out is exactly the spec.yaml use case: excerpts inside test files accompanying software are explicitly permitted. Code Components (Web IDL, markup, code examples) fall under the permissive Software License.
- The **W3C Software and Document License (2023)** ([w3.org/copyright/software-license-2023](https://www.w3.org/copyright/software-license-2023/); OSI-approved, [opensource.org/license/w3c](https://opensource.org/license/w3c)) permits copy/modify/distribute "for any purpose and without fee or royalty" with notice retention. Most W3C Editor's Drafts maintained on GitHub (CSSWG drafts, many WG repos) use this license — those can be vendored outright.
- W3C **URI persistence pledge** ([w3.org/Consortium/Persistence.html](https://www.w3.org/Consortium/Persistence.html)): dated `/TR/` URLs ("Those which start 'https://www.w3.org/TR/' immediately followed by four decimal digits") are persistent resources that "will continue to be available throughout the life of the Consortium"; even on dissolution "any website will be granted the right to make a copy … of all public persistent resources so long as they are not modified and are preserved in their entirety."

**WHATWG — CC BY 4.0.** All WHATWG Living Standards (HTML, DOM, …) switched to Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 in 2017 ([blog.whatwg.org/copyright-license-change](https://blog.whatwg.org/copyright-license-change); [whatwg/html LICENSE](https://github.com/whatwg/html/blob/main/LICENSE)), with source-code portions BSD-3-Clause. CC BY permits copying, modification, and redistribution with attribution → WHATWG spec text **can be vendored into an Apache-2.0 repo** (attribution note required; CC BY content stays under CC BY).

**OASIS (XLIFF 2.x, ODF).** The standard OASIS copyright notice on every published spec: "This document and translations of it may be copied and furnished to others, and derivative works that **comment on or otherwise explain it or assist in its implementation** may be prepared, copied, published and distributed, **in whole or in part, without restriction of any kind**, provided that the above copyright notice and this paragraph are included on all such copies and derivative works. However, this document itself may not be modified in any way…" ([OASIS notices example](https://docs.oasis-open.org/dita/dita/v1.3/os/part2-tech-content/resources/oasis-notices.html); IPR policy: [oasis-open.org/policies-guidelines/ipr](https://www.oasis-open.org/policies-guidelines/ipr/)). Unmodified copies and implementation-assisting excerpts (test files, docs) are fine with notice retention. OASIS publication URLs are versioned and stage-frozen forever: e.g. XLIFF 2.1 OS lives permanently at [docs.oasis-open.org/xliff/xliff-core/v2.1/os/xliff-core-v2.1-os.html](https://docs.oasis-open.org/xliff/xliff-core/v2.1/os/xliff-core-v2.1-os.html) (pattern `docs.oasis-open.org/<tc>/<spec>/v<ver>/<stage>/…`).

**Ecma (ECMA-376 OOXML, ECMA-262).** Ecma's text copyright policy ([ecma-international.org/policies/by-ipr/ecma-text-copyright-policy](https://ecma-international.org/policies/by-ipr/ecma-text-copyright-policy/)): default notice — "This document may be copied, published and distributed to others" with the notice intact; the content "may not be modified in any way" except translation/format conversion; derivative works limited to commentary, accessibility, translation, and implementations. Standards download free of charge ([ECMA-376 page](https://ecma-international.org/publications-and-standards/standards/ecma-376/)); Ecma states publications "can be freely copied by all interested parties without copyright restrictions." Public archival copies exist as precedent (e.g. [MIT mirror of ECMA-376](https://web.mit.edu/~stevenj/www/ecma376.html)). Newer Ecma standards add a BSD license over code/pseudo-code portions. **Practical upshot: ECMA-376 PDFs (≈5000 pp Part 1) can be cached, vendored unmodified, and excerpted with notice — making the ISO/IEC 29500 paywall irrelevant for OOXML.**

**ISO/IEC (29500, 26300) — the hard no.** ISO copyright page: any use including copying "in whole or in part, for example to another Internet site" requires written permission ([iso.org/copyright.html](https://www.iso.org/copyright.html)). The official user guide *How to best use IEC and ISO standards* ([PUB100206 PDF](https://www.iso.org/files/live/sites/isoorg/files/store/en/PUB100206.pdf)) describes purchased PDFs as **single-user licenses, copying and networking prohibited, internal use only**; permission to cite/excerpt in products is granted "on a case-by-case basis." National bodies publish concrete excerpt ceilings (e.g. Finland's SFS: max ~20%/20 pages, and only in teaching materials — [sfs.fi](https://sfs.fi/en/keep-in-mind-the-correct-use-of-standards-pay-attention-to-copyright/)). Both ISO/IEC 29500 and 26300 have free, technically-equivalent twins (ECMA-376; OASIS ODF) — use those exclusively.

**IETF RFCs — most permissive text corpus.** Trust Legal Provisions (TLP-5, [trustee.ietf.org/documents/trust-legal-provisions/tlp-5](https://trustee.ietf.org/documents/trust-legal-provisions/tlp-5/)): anyone may "publish and translate unmodified IETF Documents … for any purpose" ([TLP FAQ](https://trustee.ietf.org/documents/trust-legal-provisions/copyright-policy-and-tlp-faq/)). RFC 8721: "There is rough consensus that quoting without modification of excerpts from IETF Contributions is permitted … such excerpts being **of any length** and in any context", with attribution ([rfc-editor / ietf.org RFC 8721](https://www.ietf.org/rfc/rfc8721.html)). Code Components are BSD. Modification/derivatives outside the standards process are *not* granted — vendor verbatim only.

**Unicode (UAX/UTS, CLDR, LDML, MF2).** Two regimes ([licensing policy](https://www.unicode.org/policies/licensing_policy.html)): (a) **Unicode License v3** ([unicode.org/license.txt](https://www.unicode.org/license.txt), SPDX `Unicode-3.0`, OSI-approved 2023 — [opensource.org/license/unicode-license-v3](https://opensource.org/license/unicode-license-v3)) covers **data files and software** (UCD, CLDR data, ICU): MIT-like, fully redistributable/modifiable with notice. (b) The **Unicode Terms of Use** cover the standard's prose (core spec, UAX/UTS reports, code charts): "freely available for personal and internal business use while being subject to certain restrictions on modification and public distribution." So: CLDR/UCD data → vendor freely; UAX/UTS prose → cache privately + excerpt with citation; avoid republishing whole reports. (MessageFormat 2 / LDML live in unicode-org GitHub repos under the Unicode license — check each repo's LICENSE; the data/spec sources there are vendorable.)

**Microsoft Open Specifications ([MS-DOCX] etc.).** Every [MS-*] doc carries the IPR Notice (verbatim from [MS-DOCX]): "**you can make copies of it in order to develop implementations** of the technologies that are described in this documentation and **can distribute portions of it in your implementations … or in your documentation as necessary to properly document the implementation**. You can also distribute in your implementation, with or without modification, any schemas, IDLs, or code samples … This permission also applies to any documents that are referenced in the Open Specifications documentation." Plus "No Trade Secrets," and patents covered (for listed specs) by the [Open Specifications Promise](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/openspecs/dev_center/ms-devcentlp/1c24c7c8-28b0-4ce1-a47d-95fe1ff504bc) — a covenant not to sue conformant implementations ([Wikipedia overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Open_Specification_Promise)). Source: [[MS-DOCX] landing page](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/openspecs/office_standards/ms-docx/b839fe1f-e1ca-4fa6-8c26-5954d0abbccd). Implementation copies → private cache clearly fine; portions in documentation/tests fine. Bonus: each [MS-*] page lists **dated revision downloads** (`[MS-DOCX]-251113.docx`, every revision back to 2009) and an RSS feed for updates — built-in pinning + watch mechanism.

**Google developer docs (Android resources, ARB via Flutter docs).** developers.google.com content is **CC BY 4.0**, code samples Apache-2.0 ([Site Policies](https://developers.google.com/terms/site-policies)) → vendorable with attribution.

**Apple developer docs (xcstrings, .strings, .stringsdict).** No general reuse license: Apple "retains ownership of all its rights in the Site, Content … no other rights or licenses are granted" ([Apple Developer terms](https://developer.apple.com/support/terms/); [Developer Agreement PDF](https://developer.apple.com/support/downloads/terms/apple-developer-agreement/Apple-Developer-Agreement-20250318-English.pdf)). **Link-only + short fair-use quotes.** Apple formats also have no formal spec — observed-behavior fixtures carry more weight than citations here.

**Community/GitHub-hosted specs.** CommonMark spec is **CC-BY-SA 4.0** ([LICENSE](https://github.com/commonmark/commonmark-spec/blob/master/LICENSE)); its `spec.txt` *is* the test corpus — examples export as JSON `{markdown, html, section, number}` with `spec_tests.py` ([repo](https://github.com/commonmark/commonmark-spec)); versioned permanent URLs per release ([spec.commonmark.org/0.27/](https://spec.commonmark.org/0.27/), [changelog](https://spec.commonmark.org/changelog.txt)). TOML (MIT), Fluent (Apache-2.0), JSON Schema, DTCG design tokens (W3C Community Group Final Specification Agreement → permissive): each repo's LICENSE governs; pin by git SHA/submodule. CC-BY-SA caveat: ShareAlike triggers only on *adaptations* — verbatim vendored spec.txt + attribution is fine inside an Apache-2.0 repo as a separately-licensed file.

### 2. Spec text in LLM prompts/RAG and quotes in spec.yaml

- **US Copyright Office, *Copyright and AI, Part 3: Generative AI Training* (pre-publication, May 9, 2025)** ([copyright.gov PDF](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-3-Generative-AI-Training-Report-Pre-Publication-Version.pdf)): fair use is fact-specific; uses "for purposes of noncommercial research or analysis that do not enable portions of the works to be reproduced in the outputs are likely to be fair"; **RAG "involves the reproduction of copyrighted works" and is less likely transformative when outputs deliver the work's expressive content** (analyses: [Skadden](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/05/copyright-office-report), [Debevoise, June 2025](https://www.debevoisedatablog.com/2025/06/04/preliminary-copyright-office-guidance-on-fair-use-and-ai-provides-some-answers-but-questions-remain/)). For neokapi: a *private* RAG cache used to generate *tests* (functional output, no spec prose in the product beyond short cited quotes) sits on the strongly-favored end — closed system, non-substitutive task, factual/functional source material.
- For ISO specifically the binding constraint is **contract, not copyright**: the purchased-PDF single-user license prohibits copying/networking ([PUB100206](https://www.iso.org/files/live/sites/isoorg/files/store/en/PUB100206.pdf)), so loading purchased ISO text into a shared CI cache or third-party LLM API is a license-breach risk regardless of fair use. Avoid by sourcing the Ecma/OASIS twins.
- Short normative quotes with citation are classic fair use (small amount; factual/functional nature; zero market substitution); the IETF FAQ notes "copyright law does not generally protect small fragments of text or code that, in themselves, do not evidence creative expression" ([TLP FAQ](https://trustee.ietf.org/documents/trust-legal-provisions/copyright-policy-and-tlp-faq/)).
- **OSS practice quotes far less than neokapi plans to**: test262 cites by **anchor only** — required `esid:` frontmatter holding the spec's HTML fragment id, e.g. `esid: sec-typedarray-length` ([CONTRIBUTING.md](https://github.com/tc39/test262/blob/main/CONTRIBUTING.md)); WPT cites by `<link rel=help href=…>` URL, enforced by the lint ([general guidelines](https://web-platform-tests.org/writing-tests/general-guidelines.html)); CommonMark embeds full examples but owns its spec. neokapi's `{url, anchor, quote}` triple is *more* defensible than industry practice as long as quotes stay short (one normative sentence, ≲25–50 words) and the per-publisher matrix below is respected.

### 3. Prior art: machine-readable spec catalogs and extraction infrastructure

- **w3c/browser-specs** ([repo](https://github.com/w3c/browser-specs), [README schema](https://github.com/w3c/browser-specs/blob/main/README.md)): curated `specs.json` → built `index.json` with per-spec `shortname`, `url` (canonical, "mostly stable"), `series.{shortname,currentSpecification}`, `seriesVersion`, `seriesComposition` (full/delta/fork), `release.url` (dated /TR/ snapshot), `nightly.url` ("the least stable identifier"), `nightly.repository`/`sourcePath`, `tests.repository`+`testPaths`, `standing`, `obsoletedBy`, `formerNames` (renames tracked since 2023). Freshness: `npm run build` pulls from official APIs (W3C API, WHATWG workstreams DB, IETF datatracker); GitHub workflows auto-open pre-release PRs; published to npm as `web-specs`/`browser-specs`.
- **w3c/webref + w3c/reffy** ([webref](https://github.com/w3c/webref), [reffy](https://github.com/w3c/reffy)): crawler runs **every 6 hours** for nightlies, daily for /TR/; raw extracts in `ed/` — **`ed/ids` (every fragment defined in a spec), `ed/headings`, `ed/dfns` (linking text, type, namespace), `ed/links` (outbound links + targeted fragments)** — one JSON file per spec shortname. A **`curated` branch applies manually-maintained patches** to raw data automatically "unless patches need to be modified (which requires manual intervention)"; reviewed subsets ship weekly as `@webref/*` npm packages. This raw→patch→curated pipeline is the canonical pattern for keeping extracted spec data fresh with bounded human effort.
- **specref** ([github.com/tobie/specref](https://github.com/tobie/specref), api at specref.org): community bibliographic DB mapping spec ids → canonical metadata — the lightweight precedent for a `catalog` file.
- **Bikeshed** ([docs](https://speced.github.io/bikeshed/)): auto-generates heading/dfn IDs from text ([§ Automatic ID Generation](https://speced.github.io/bikeshed/#id-gen)); supports an `oldids` attribute so renamed anchors keep resolving, and a **`Required IDs` metadata check**: "a list of IDs that the document *must* contain. This can be used to ensure that certain links into the document don't rot over time." That is a spec-side CI lint for anchor stability — the mirror image of the citation-side lint neokapi needs.
- **WPT lint** ([lint-tool docs](https://web-platform-tests.org/writing-tests/lint-tool.html)): MISSING-LINK errors when a CSS test lacks `<link rel=help>`; runs on every PR; reviewers won't merge with lint errors. Precedent: *citation presence* is machine-enforced at review time.
- **MDN browser-compat-data**: `spec_url` is mandatory for standards-track features and **"Each URL must contain a fragment identifier (a deep link)"** ([compat-data schema](https://github.com/mdn/browser-compat-data/blob/main/schemas/compat-data-schema.md)); [w3c/mdn-spec-links](https://github.com/w3c/mdn-spec-links) maintains the reverse map fragment-id → MDN page. Precedent: fragment-bearing URLs as the citation atom, validated by schema.

### 4. Anchor stability across revisions

- **W3C dated TR URLs + fragments are effectively immutable** (persistence pledge above) — cite the dated URL, not the latest-version alias.
- **WHATWG living standards churn**; pin with **commit snapshots** (`https://html.spec.whatwg.org/commit-snapshots/<sha>/`) so `{snapshot-url, #fragment}` is frozen.
- **CommonMark**: each release is permanently archived at `spec.commonmark.org/<version>/` with section anchors and **example numbers stable within a version but renumbered across versions** ([changelog](https://spec.commonmark.org/changelog.txt)) — citation key must be `{version, section, example#}`, never bare example numbers.
- **OASIS**: stage URLs frozen; **section numbers can shift between versions** (XLIFF 2.0→2.1→2.2) → version belongs in the key.
- **ISO/Ecma clause numbering** shifts across editions (ECMA-376 1st→5th ed.) → record edition + clause + heading text.
- Robust composite key, validated by the BCD/test262/webref experience: **`{spec-id, version/snapshot, anchor-or-clause, heading-text, quote-hash}`** with two fallbacks when an anchor 404s: (1) look up the anchor in the *new* version's `ids` extract (webref-style) and in any `oldids`; (2) full-text search for the quoted sentence (quote-hash match) to relocate and propose an updated anchor. webref's `ed/ids` diffs between crawls are precisely the data needed to detect anchor breakage *before* CI red.
- Citation permanence backstop: Wayback Machine snapshots as secondary URL (see §5); MDN/caniuse cite living-standard fragments and simply absorb churn via lint+fix cycles — neokapi should automate the fix proposal instead.

### 5. Snapshot/versioning infrastructure

- **Wayback SPN2 API** (`https://web.archive.org/save`, authenticated; [API docs gist](https://gist.github.com/regstuff/82e690db2f1d91ba59f6681c1abad6cf), [archive.org API page](https://archive.org/help/wayback_api.php)) — programmatic archiving at citation-creation time; perma.cc as the library-grade alternative for must-not-rot links.
- **Local content-addressed cache is the only layer fully under your control**: store fetched bytes keyed by sha256 with `meta.yaml` (`source-url`, `fetched-at`, `sha256`, `wayback-url`, `license-class`). 
- Publisher-native pinning: W3C dated /TR/ URLs; WHATWG commit snapshots; OASIS stage URLs; CommonMark version URLs; **[MS-*] dated revision artifacts** (`[MS-DOCX]-251113.docx`, full history table + RSS feed per doc — [MS-DOCX page](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/openspecs/office_standards/ms-docx/b839fe1f-e1ca-4fa6-8c26-5954d0abbccd)); GitHub-hosted specs pin by commit SHA. A watch job diffs `catalog.yaml` pinned versions against publisher feeds (W3C API, MS RSS, GitHub releases, CLDR releases) and opens an issue per bump.

### 6. Specs → retrieval units for LLM test generation

- **HTML specs**: split by heading id directly from the DOM (what reffy does); pandoc per-section HTML→markdown. webref's `ed/headings`+`ed/ids` give the section inventory for free for W3C/WHATWG specs.
- **PDF specs** (ECMA-376 Part 1 ≈5000 pp): **marker** ([datalab-to/marker](https://github.com/datalab-to/marker)) is the 2026 default for spec-grade PDF→markdown (layout analysis, reading order, tables; split very long PDFs into chunks; GPU throughput to 122 pp/s on H100); **docling** (IBM) for multi-format enterprise pipelines; MinerU for CJK; **grobid is a poor fit** for standards (converts formulas/tables to images — [arXiv 2512.18122, Dec 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.18122v1)). 2026 comparison: [Marker vs Docling vs MinerU](https://themenonlab.blog/blog/best-open-source-pdf-to-markdown-tools-2026). After conversion, split on the numbered-clause regex (`^#+\s*\d+(\.\d+)*\s`) — OOXML clause numbering is rigorous enough for mechanical splitting.
- **RAG over standards, 2024–2026**: Telco-RAG for 3GPP ([arXiv 2404.15939](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.15939), Apr 2024) — glossary-enhanced queries + tuned chunking for standards prose; fine-tuned RAG with "forward-looking semantic chunking and re-ranking" for 3GPP ([arXiv 2408.11775](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.11775), Aug 2024); agentic multimodal retrieval (TelcoAI, [arXiv 2601.16984](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.16984), Jan 2026). **iPanda** ([arXiv 2507.00378](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.00378), Jul 2025) is the closest analog to neokapi's loop: LLM agent doing protocol conformance testing — keyword-based test-case generation from protocol documents, code-oriented RAG over the implementation under test, CoT + iterative self-correction; improves test-program Pass@1 by 4.7–10.8× over raw LLM. Consensus across these: **section-anchored chunks (one unit = one numbered clause with its heading path) beat fixed-size embedding chunks for standards**, and hybrid keyword+semantic retrieval wins on terminology-dense text.
- Storage convention that works for agents: one markdown file per section with provenance frontmatter, loaded just-in-time by clause id (no vector DB needed at neokapi's corpus size; ripgrep over headings + a small keyword index suffices, with embeddings optional later).

## Design implications for neokapi

### (b) Knowledge-base layout (browser-specs + webref pattern, scaled down)

```
specs/
  catalog.yaml                      # one entry per governing spec (browser-specs-style):
                                    #   id, title, org, formats:[json,…], license-class (vendor|cache|excerpt|link),
                                    #   url (canonical), version (pinned), versioned_url (dated/TR|stage|commit-snapshot|MS dated),
                                    #   watch: {feed|api|repo}, tests_upstream (e.g. commonmark spec.txt, test262 path)
  snapshots/<spec-id>/<version>/    # vendor-class: committed; cache-class: .gitignored, fetched by make target
    source.(html|pdf|txt)           # unmodified original + required notice file
    meta.yaml                       # sha256, fetched_at, wayback_url, license, publisher_version_label
  sections/<spec-id>/<version>/     # extraction units, one .md per clause/anchor
    <anchor-or-clause>.md           # frontmatter: {spec, version, anchor, clause, heading_path, source_sha256, license-class}
```
- Keep `catalog.yaml` hand-curated like `specs.json`, with a build step that resolves live metadata (W3C API, GitHub, MS RSS) — never hand-maintain what an API serves.
- Sections for cache-class publishers (Unicode prose, Apple) live only in the local/CI cache, regenerated deterministically from `snapshots/` by the extraction toolchain; the repo commits only the *catalog + citations*, not the text.

### (c) Citation schema + CI lint

spec.yaml citation record:
```yaml
cite:
  spec: xliff-2.1            # catalog id
  version: "2.1-os"          # pinned, matches catalog
  url: https://docs.oasis-open.org/xliff/xliff-core/v2.1/os/xliff-core-v2.1-os.html#segmentation
  clause: "4.7.1"            # publisher-native numbering when anchors are weak (PDF specs)
  heading: "Segmentation"    # heading text at capture time
  quote: "…one normative sentence…"
  quote_sha256: <hash of normalized quote>
```
CI lint (mirrors WPT MISSING-LINK + BCD fragment rule + Bikeshed Required IDs, inverted):
1. every spec.yaml case has a `cite` with a fragment or clause (BCD: "Each URL must contain a fragment identifier");
2. resolve `url` against the **pinned snapshot** (not the network) — anchor must exist in the snapshot's extracted `ids` index; quote_sha256 must match text found inside that section;
3. a separate scheduled **watch job** (not PR-blocking) re-crawls live URLs, diffs the `ids`/headings index per spec (webref-style raw→curated, with a patches dir for known publisher quirks), and on breakage runs the fallback: search the new version for the quote text, propose `{new anchor, new clause}` as an auto-PR — this is the self-improving loop; the human only adjudicates the diff.

### (d) Extraction toolchain

- HTML specs (W3C/WHATWG/OASIS HTML editions, vendor docs): fetch pinned snapshot → DOM-split on heading ids (reffy-style) → pandoc to markdown per section. Reuse webref's published `ed/ids`/`ed/headings` for any spec it already covers (HTML, TTML, ITS) instead of crawling yourself.
- PDF specs (ECMA-376, some OASIS PDFs, [MS-*] PDFs — though [MS-*] also ships DOCX, which neokapi's own openxml reader can ingest, a nice dogfood): **marker** for PDF→markdown, then clause-regex splitting; docling as fallback for tables-heavy parts; skip grobid.
- Retrieval for agents: ripgrep over `sections/` heading paths + clause ids, just-in-time loading of single-clause markdown into context (iPanda/Telco-RAG result: section-anchored + keyword-first beats naive embedding chunks on standards text).

### (e) Red lines

1. **ISO/IEC text never enters the repo, the shared CI cache, or any prompt** — substitute ECMA-376 for ISO/IEC 29500 and OASIS ODF for ISO/IEC 26300 (technically equivalent, free, copyable). If an ISO-only spec ever becomes load-bearing, a purchased PDF stays on the maintainer's machine; the single-user license ([PUB100206](https://www.iso.org/files/live/sites/isoorg/files/store/en/PUB100206.pdf)) prohibits networking/sharing, and even prompts to a third-party API are contract-grey — don't build process on it.
2. **No re-hosted spec-like derivatives** of W3C/OASIS/Ecma documents (their derivative-as-spec prohibitions) — `sections/` files must be clearly excerpts with provenance frontmatter, never assembled back into a browseable mirror of an excerpt-class spec.
3. **Apple: link + short quote only**; never vendor or republish; lean on fixtures/oracles, not citation text.
4. **Quotes stay short and normative** (≤ one sentence/~50 words), always with `{url, anchor}` — exceeding industry practice (test262/WPT cite anchors only) is fine, but the quote field must never become a section dump.
5. **CC-BY-SA material (CommonMark) stays in clearly separately-licensed files** with its LICENSE alongside; never blend its text into Apache-2.0-licensed source files beyond short attributed quotes.
6. **Pin or don't cite**: a citation against a living/latest URL without a pinned snapshot in `catalog.yaml` fails lint — the resolvability guarantee only holds against bytes you control (snapshot + sha256 + optional Wayback secondary URL).
